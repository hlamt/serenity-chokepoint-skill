# Source Types and Evidence Strength

This reference defines source types for supply-chain chokepoint research. Every evidence item should label its source type before it is used in a research hypothesis.

| Source type | Definition | Examples | Evidence strength | Risks | Can independently support a chokepoint claim? |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Primary public expression | A public statement by the original framework source or original author, including Serenity public expression when provenance is clear. | A short user-provided excerpt from a public post; a public interview clip with attribution; an original public note. | Medium for framework interpretation; low to medium for factual industry claims unless independently verified. | May be time-bound, rhetorical, abbreviated, translated incorrectly, or taken out of context. | No for a true supply-chain chokepoint claim; it can frame a hypothesis but needs industry-chain evidence. |
| Company disclosure | Public company materials released by the company. | Annual report, sustainability report, investor presentation, product release, capacity announcement. | Medium to high for what the company says about itself. | Selective disclosure, promotional framing, lag, non-comparable metrics, omission of customer or yield details. | Sometimes, if paired with demand, capacity, substitution, and customer evidence; rarely alone. |
| Regulatory filing | Mandatory filing submitted to a regulator or exchange. | Prospectus, 10-K, 20-F, exchange announcement, securities filing, import/export license record. | High for disclosed facts within the filing scope. | Legal language may be conservative, delayed, jurisdiction-specific, or incomplete for operating reality. | Sometimes for existence, capacity, risk, and dependency claims; still needs cross-checks for chokepoint persistence and value capture. |
| Earnings call transcript | Management and analyst discussion around financial results. | Prepared remarks, Q&A transcript, conference transcript. | Medium for management commentary and near-term operating signals. | Optimistic framing, selective answers, analyst assumptions, transcription errors. | Not alone; can support a hypothesis when cross-checked with filings, customers, capacity, and market data. |
| Industry report | Third-party research on markets, capacity, technology, or supply chains. | Industry association report, consultant report, technical roadmap, supply-demand review. | Medium, depending on methodology and transparency. | Methodology opacity, sponsor bias, stale estimates, broad aggregation. | Sometimes for market structure; not alone for company-specific value capture. |
| Market data | Quantitative price, volume, shipment, inventory, utilization, or trade data. | ASP series, import/export data, shipment data, utilization data, inventory indicators. | Medium to high for observed market movement when provenance is clear. | Data revisions, sampling bias, licensing limits, noisy proxies, correlation mistaken for causation. | Not alone; it can indicate pressure but needs mechanism evidence. |
| News article | Journalistic report about companies, industries, policy, or supply-demand events. | Reputable business press, trade press, local media with named sources. | Low to medium, depending on sourcing. | Anonymous sourcing, speed errors, headline exaggeration, single-source narratives. | No alone; use as lead generation or corroboration. |
| Social media post | Public post on a social platform, including X, when lawfully supplied or collected under an explicit authorization gate. | User-provided post excerpt; public thread link; public technical discussion. | Low for factual claims; medium for direct self-expression if original and attributed. | Virality, impersonation, deletion, missing context, screenshots without provenance. | No; social media virality is not evidence of a true supply-chain chokepoint. |
| Community interpretation | Secondary interpretation, translation, summary, or commentary by a community member. | Chinese community summary of Serenity; forum explanation; newsletter recap. | Low for original claims; useful for mapping discourse. | Misquotation, translation drift, motivated interpretation, loss of nuance. | No; must not be treated as original Serenity expression or industry evidence. |
| User-provided note | Material supplied by the user that may include observations, excerpts, or internal analysis without independent verification. | User pasted note, spreadsheet summary, meeting note, manual excerpt. | Unverified until source, date, and provenance are checked. | Private/confidential material, missing permission, incomplete provenance, user bias. | No alone; can define a question or lead, but needs traceable public evidence. |

## Evidence use rule

A durable supply-chain chokepoint hypothesis should normally combine multiple source types: primary or company evidence for the mechanism, market or industry data for pressure, cross-source checks for persistence, and explicit counterevidence for substitution, expansion, demand reversal, and constraint migration.
